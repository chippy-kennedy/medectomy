Outer Structure

            Non-enveloped RNA viruses consist of an icosahedral protein capsid which contains a viral genome.  Since they do not have an outer, lipid-bilayer envelope they are less sensitive to detergents and drying.  Due to this, non-enveloped RNA viruses have longer survival times on fomites and within the GI tract.  Therefore, a common method of non-enveloped RNA viral transmission is the fecal-oral route.  As a result of their ability to survive within the GI tract, they are also more likely to cause gastrointestinal infections.1

            Since non-enveloped RNA viruses do not need to acquire a lipid-envelope during their exit from host cells, they tend to leave cells via lysis as opposed to budding.  This exit method also makes non-enveloped RNA viruses more susceptible to attack by humoral immunity.2    

Genome

            As with enveloped RNA viruses, non-enveloped RNA viruses have a variety of different genomic characteristics which can be utilized as distinguishing features.  Firstly, the majority of non-enveloped RNA viruses consist of single-stranded, linear RNA.  The exception to this rule is the Reovirus Family, which has a double-stranded RNA genome.  Secondly, the majority of non-enveloped RNA viruses also consist of non-segmented genomes.  Once again, the exception to this rule is the Reovirus Family, which has a segmented genome.

            Unlike the enveloped RNA viruses, the non-enveloped RNA viruses all consist of positive sense RNA genomes.  As discussed in the chapter on enveloped RNA viruses, RNA viruses with a positive sense genome are coded in the 5’-3’ direction (identical in orientation to human mRNA) and can be easily translated by host ribosomes upon host-cell infiltration.  Due to this, positive sense RNA genomes are often referred to as “infectious” because insertion of the viral genetic material itself can lead to viral replication.3

 

 

 

Non-enveloped RNA Viral Families

            Overall, there are less non-enveloped RNA viruses than there are enveloped RNA viruses.  The RNA viral families which are non-enveloped include the following: Reovirus Family, Picornavirus Family, Hepevirus Family, Calicivirus Family.  The rest of this review chapter will focus upon each family individually, and the diseases which they cause. 

1.        The Reovirus Family

The members of the Reovirus family have linear, double-stranded RNA with 10-12 segments. The two primary members of the Reovirus family which cause disease in humans are the Coltivirus and the Rotavirus.

The disease caused by the Coltivirus in humans in known as Colorado Tick Fever.  It is characterized by a self-limited disease with a mild fever, myalgias, arthralgias, and rarely encephalitis.  Infections by the Coltivirus are usually sporadic, and outbreaks are uncommon. 

Unlike the Coltivirus, the Rotavirus is responsible for a significant number of annual outbreaks in humans.  Overall, the Rotavirus causes a severe gastrointestinal infection in infants and children from the ages of 6 months to 2 years old.  Infections by the Rotavirus present with large amounts of watery diarrhea and a lack of emesis. 

Due to its segmented genome, the Rotavirus is highly variable--making the establishment of long-term immunity difficult. Therefore, outbreaks happen among susceptible populations annually, with reinfection occurring from year-to-year.  Overall, it is the number one cause of fatal diarrhea in infants worldwide.4

Like all unenveloped RNA viruses, the Rotavirus is resistant to drying and is commonly transmitted via the fecal-oral route.  Therefore, general hygiene is important in preventing Rotavirus spread.  A common hotbed for Rotavirus transmission are kindergartens and daycares.

In addition to general hygiene, there exists a live-attenuated vaccine which can offer protection against Rotavirus infection.  The vaccine is unique in that it uses viral segment reassortment as a form of attenuation: mixing bovine Rotaviral segments with human Rotaviral segments in order to decrease virulence and confer a strong Th1 and Th2 immune protection to recipients.5

2.        The Picornavirus Family

The members of the Picornavirus family contain single-stranded, non-segmented, positive-sense genomes.  Of the various RNA viruses, they are the smallest.  Overall, the Picornavirus Family contains a wide variety of human pathogens.  They are as follows: Poliovirus, Echovirus, Rhinovirus, Coxsackievirus A/B Virus, and the Hepatitis A Virus.6

The Poliovirus has been an important human pathogen for generations.  As with most non-enveloped RNA viruses, the Poliovirus is highly contagious, and before the development of the Polio vaccine massive outbreaks of the virus would occur during the summer worldwide.  Most times the Poliovirus is transmitted via fecal-oral contact, and replication occurs within the oropharynx and gut. 

Depending on the patient, the Poliovirus can cause diseases of differeing symptamology and severity.  In 90% of individuals the Poliovirus causes an asymptomatic illness where the virus merely replicates in the GI tact and is shed by the host.  In 5-7% of individuals the Poliovirus causes a flulike illness which is self-limiting and can sometimes progress into an aseptic meningitis.  The least common form of Polio infection is also the most deadly.  In this form of Poliovirus infection, deemed Paralytic Poliomyelitis, an initial Polio replication within the gut leads to a viremia which migrates to the anterior horn neurons of the spinal cord.  Infection and inflammation within the anterior horn leads to lower motor neuron paralysis.  Sometimes the virus migrates far enough rostrally to affect the cranial nerves and medulla--resulting in life-threatening Bulbar Polio and possible respiratory paralysis. 

Some previous sufferers of paralytic Polio go on to develop Post-polio Syndrome decades later in life.  This syndrome causes a weakening of the originally affected muscles which is believed to be due to a decreased number of neurons in the innervating nerves. 

Two major forms of vaccine exist for the Poliovirus, both of which are trivalent.  One of the vaccines for Poliovirus is taken orally, and consists of live attenuated strains of the virus.  Traditionally this vaccine is referred to as the Sabin Vaccine after its creator.  Although the Sabin Vaccine confers excellent protection against Polio infection, it sometimes results in the formation of active infection due to revertant mutants.  In this circumstance, vaccinated individuals may develop an asymptomatic infection which can be transmitted to unvaccinated individuals, resulting in active infection.  This is of most concern in developing nations where the Sabin Vaccine is still utilized. 

The other form of polio vaccine consists of inactivated virus.  It is administered intramuscularly, and is referred to as the Salk Vaccine (again, after its creator).  Overall, the Salk Vaccine is the only polio vaccination which is given within the first world, including the United States.7,8 

Another common Picornavirus which affects humans is the Echovirus.  Although the Echovirus may be hard to diagnose clinically, it is the most common cause of aseptic meningitis in infants and children.9 

The Rhinovirus is an extremely prevalent form of human Picornavirus.  With several hundred documented strains, the Rhinovirus is ubiquitous within humans.  It is the most prevalent cause of the “common cold”--a self-limiting upper respiratory tract infection which is stereotyped by rhinorrhea, the rare presence of fever, and post-nasal drip.  As with the other picornaviruses it is extremely contagious, and outbreaks happen seasonally.  Unlike the other picornaviruses, the Rhinovirus is sensitive to high temperatures and low pH, therefore explaining the isolation of its infection to the upper respiratory tract.10 

The Coxsackieviruses are another form of Picornavirus which infect humans.  Overall, the human Coxsackieviruses are differentiated into strains A and B.  Although both strains can cause aseptic meningitis, Coxsackie A and B cause relatively different individual diseases. 

The two main diseases caused by Coxsackie A are Herpangina and Hand-foot-and-mouth Disease.  Herpangina is characterized by fever, pain on swallowing, and vomiting.  Its hallmark feature is the presence of ulcerating vesicular lesions around the uvula and soft palate.  The disease is self-limiting and non-fatal.  Hand-foot-and-mouth Disease is characterized by the presence of vesicular lesions on the hands, feet, and oral cavity.  In some cases the patient may have a mild fever, but the infection is self-limiting and subsides within a week. 

Coxsackie B can cause Pleurodynia or viral myocarditis.  Also referred to as Bornholm Disease or the “Devil’s Grip,” Pleurodynia is characterized by rapid onset of fever and unilateral, excruciating, pleuritic chest pain.  Vomiting and abdominal pain may also be present in Pleurodynia, but the illness often self-resolves within several days.  Despite this, relapses of the pleuritic chest pain within days of resolution are common. 

Coxsackie B Myocarditis is characterized by sudden-onset symptoms of heart failure in children and young people.  In a small amount of cases pericarditis with friction rub may also be present.  This is the most life-threatening form of Coxsackie infection, and it demands immediate symptomatic treatment to diminish the impact of heart failure as the body develops and immune response.  Despite intervention, a small number of Coxsackie B Myocarditis infections can result in the development of a subsequent cardiomyopathy and heart failure.9 

The final member of the Picornavirus Family which infects humans is the Hepatitis A Virus (HAV).  Unlike Hepatitis B, Hepatitis A is transmitted via the fecal-oral route as opposed to the parenteral/sexual route.  It has a relatively short incubation period (3 weeks) when compared to other hepatitis viruses, and although it only causes “acute” hepatitis the majority of patients only have an asymptomatic infection.  In addition this, infection with the virus is rarely fatal.

HAV is normally transmitted via contaminated food--leading to occasional outbreaks from restaurants and food stands.  In addition to contaminated foods, daycare and school workers are at risk of contracting HAV from exposure to fecal matter from children.  With this in mind, hygiene plays an important role in preventing HAV transmission.  However, an inactivated vaccine has also been developed to protect individuals who are at risk of exposure to Hepatitis A.11 

3. The Hepevirus Family

The members of the Hepevirus Family contain single-stranded, non-segmented, positive-sense genomes.  The major member of the Hepevirus Family which is disease-causing in humans is the Hepatitis E Virus. 

Like the Hepatitis A Virus, the Hepatitis E Virus is transmitted by the fecal-oral route.  Therefore, it is commonly contracted via contact with contaminated food.  Unlike HAV, HEV is a common source of water-borne epidemics, especially in the developing world.  It is very similar to HAV in terms of its course and symptamology--resulting in an acute hepatitis which is rarely fatal.  However, Hepatitis E Virus is extremely dangerous in pregnant women--resulting in a high mortality rate.11,12

3.        The Calicivirus Family

The members of the Calicivirus Family contain single-stranded, non-segmented, positive-sense, RNA genomes.  The main disease-causing member of the Calicivirus Family in humans is the Norovirus. 

As with other non-enveloped RNA viruses, the Norovirus is primarily transmitted via the fecal-oral route.  Within humans, its infection is hallmarked by acute gastrointestinal symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain.  Fever may also be present.  The Norovirus can be differentiated from the Rotavirus (another cause of acute GI distress) due to the presence of vomiting and the fact that it tends to infect older children and adults.  Outbreaks of the Norovirus tend to happen yearly worldwide, and they commonly occur in areas where individuals are in close proximity with one another.  Due to this, Norovirus outbreaks are common within households and on cruise ships.  By and large, Norovirus infections are self-limiting and resolve within several days.  Viral shedding continues for several weeks following infection, however.13
