Chapter 2: Classification of Viruses

As with bacteria, there is spectrum of different viruses that are pathogenic to humans. The classification systems that exist for viruses consider their nucleic acid composition, their capsid symmetry, or the presence or absence of a viral envelope. In this chapter, we discuss these three methods of classifying viruses separately, and then provide summary tables as a means of organizing different viruses according to these properties.

Classification Based on Nucleic Acid Composition

All known viruses possess a genome that is composed of either RNA or DNA. (In other words, there are no known viruses that possess a genome that is a RNA/DNA hybrid.) In addition, viral genomes may be either double-stranded (ds) or single-stranded (ss). Among the single stranded viruses, the nucleic acid contained within the viral particle can be either positive-sense (+) or negative-sense (-). In addition, viruses may carry a single segment of DNA, whereas others may carry as many as 12 segments (some Reoviruses), with each segment having a unique DNA or RNA sequence. Alternatively, retroviruses are known to contain two identical copies of their nucleic acid segments, making them effectively diploid.

The Baltimore classification, first defined by David Baltimore in 1971, categorizes viruses based on the properties of their genetic material.1 It is extremely important to have an understanding of these different genome types. There are a total of seven categories, described as follows:

It is important to note that Parvovirus is the only ssDNA virus of medical significance, while Reoviruses are the only dsRNA viruses of medical importance. The majority of viruses that infect humans are either dsDNA or ssRNA.

It is important to understand the different types of viral genomes that exist because these genome types very widely in regards to their susceptibility to certain therapies, the approximate rate at which they mutate, and whether or not they are infectious in their “naked” state (without a virion surrounding the nucleic acid.)

Genome type and therapy susceptibility

As a retrovirus, HIV requires the presence of an active reverse transcriptase gene to maintain a chronic infection within a human host. Thus, many drugs used to treat HIV target this enzyme, which can serve to slow down progression of this disease to AIDS. As one might expect, viruses that do not require reverse transcriptase for survival will continue to proliferate in the presence of a drug that targets this enzyme.

Genome type and mutation rate

Generally speaking, RNA polymerase enzymes tend to be much more prone to error than DNA polymerase enzymes, due to the lack of a proof-reading domain. Therefore, one might correctly assume that RNA viruses mutate at a faster rate than DNA viruses. This serves to explain the phenomenon of antigenic drift observed in influenzaviruses, where the genome slowly evolves over time. This evolution is responsible, in part, for why the influenza vaccine varies from year to year. Additionally, reverse transcriptase also lacks a proofreading domain, much like RNA polymerase. This helps to explain why HIV is able to rapidly mutate to evade antiretroviral drugs.

Genome type and infectivity

The type of genome present in a virus can help one to deduce whether the genome itself is infective. By definition, an infective genome is one that can be directly injected into a cell to cause infection, even in the absence of any viral proteins (capsid proteins, viral translational proteins, etc.). (+)ssRNA almost always possess infective genomes, as do most dsDNA viruses. For infection, the injection of poliovirus RNA into a susceptible cell will lead to the production of poliovirus virions. Alternatively, (-)ssRNA genomes and dsRNA genomes are not infective in their naked form, as these genomes cannot produce all of the proteins necessary for viral survival and proliferation without the aid of an RNA-dependent RNA-polymerase, which is an enzyme not normally produced by the host cell.

 

Classification Based on Capsid Symmetry

The viral capsid is a proteinaceous structure that serves to encapsulate the viral genome. It is typically an oligomeric structure composed of several different capsid proteins. For example, the capsid for the poliovirus consists of a collection of three virally-encoded proteins (VP1, VP2, and VP3), which interact to form a capsid of icosahedral symmetry. Icosahedral is one of the two major geometries of viral capsids, the other being helical. In simplistic terms, a viral capsid with icosahedral symmetry vaguely resembles a soccer ball, while a viral capsid with a helical symmetry resembles a compressed spring.

 

For viruses with icosahedral symmetry, there is one icosahedron that surrounds the entire viral genome, regardless of how many viral nucleotide fragments are present. For example, in reoviruses, all ten to twelve segments of RNA are contained within a single icosahedron, which is in turn, comprised of an inner and outer protein coat. Alternatively, viruses with helical symmetry may possess more than one helix in a single virion. For example, in orthomyxoviruses, each segment of RNA is surrounded by its own helical capsid, which combine to produce a single virion.

 

There are a few viruses, notably poxviruses, that possess a capsid symmetry that is neither helical nor icosahedral. The virus that causes small pox in humans, for example, is described as “brick-like” in its physical characteristics. It possesses neither icosahedral nor helical capsid symmetry.

Classification Based on a Viral Envelope

Viruses can be described as being either enveloped or non-enveloped. When viral envelopes are present, they surround the capsid and serve as the outermost barrier between the virion and the environment. The viral envelope typically consists of lipid and glycoproteins. The glycoproteins are viral in nature, while the lipid is derived from the host membrane. Enveloped viruses bud from the host cell, rather than inducing cell lysis, and thus carry host membrane with them as the major component of their viral envelope.

Enveloped viruses tend to be more resistant to environmental factors, such as heat and desiccation, than non-enveloped viruses. In addition, the treatment of enveloped virions with detergent will separate the viral envelope from the rest of the virion, generally rendering the virion non-infective.
