Slow viruses describe a collection of infectious agents with extremely long incubation times, often on the order of years to decades. Many of these agents are viruses that cause an acute infection and then go dormant for many years before being reactivated, sometimes for reasons unknown. The characteristics of slow viruses are as follows:

(1)   Have a long incubation period (can be as long as decades).

(2)   Follow a slow but nearly inevitable course to death.

(3)   Some may have a genetic predisposition.

(4)   Some may reactivate when the patient becomes immune compromised.

The important slow viruses are described in the sections that follow.

 

 

Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy

This condition is caused by the JC virus, a polyomavirus. In immuno-competent people, this virus is innocuous, and it is believed that 40% to 60% of the population becomes seropositive during life, often in childhood or adolescence. In the immuno-compromised, including those who receive certain immune dampening chemotherapeutic agents and those with AIDS, the virus activates and runs rampant. It is unclear as to exactly why, but individuals with AIDS tend to be much more prone to this condition than individuals with other immuno-suppressive conditions.

In PML, this virus causes significant damage to the white matter of the brain. Viral destruction of oligodendrocytes slows nerve transmission due to the absence of a myelin sheath. Symptoms include paralysis, cognitive decline, and problems with vision and speech. PML is diagnosed by brain biopsy or the presence of viral DNA is CSF. The damage caused by this condition is also visible on MRI. There is, at present, no cure for this condition.

 

Subacute Sclerosing Panencephalitis

Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE) is caused by the measles virus. It typically occurs 6 to 15 years following a primary measles infection, and progresses relatively quickly (approx. 3 years) to death. Symptoms of this condition include personality changes, myoclonic jerks, seizures, coma, and eventually death. Early symptoms are unremarkable, and include a general decrease in activity, inability to focus, and tiredness.

Individuals who contracted measles naturally have an approximately 1 in 100,000 chance of developing SSPE. Alternatively, individuals who were vaccinated against measles have only a 1 in 1,000,000 chance of developing this condition.

Diagnosis typically includes a finding of increased anti-measles IgG and abnormalities noted in brain biopsies. In addition, changes are often detectable on MRI or EEG.

 

Prion Disorders

Although prions are not viruses, they are infective agents and resemble true slow viruses in many important regards. Prions are abnormally folded proteins that are capable of inducing the improper folding of other proteins, specifically within the central nervous system. The aggregation of misfolded proteins gives the brain a sponge-like appearance, which is why many of these conditions are described as being “spongiform.” Some important spongiform conditions are described below.

Kuru

Although Kuru is a condition that will probably never be encountered in the clinic, it is important for its historical value. It was observed only in a single tribe from New Guinea, the Fore people. Symptoms include behavioral changes and tremors, and its incubation period is approximately 4 to 12 years.5 The time interval from the earliest symptoms to death is about 6 to 9 months. It was noted that the brains of individuals affected by Kuru were spongiform in nature, and resembled those of sheep affected by the condition Scrapie. Ultimately, it was demonstrated that this condition was caused by the consumption of human brains, a practice that was not uncommon among the Fore people as part of their funeral ritual.

Fatal familial insomnia

Fatal familial insomnia is another prior disorder, with an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern. The course of the disease involves insomnia followed by hallucinations, delirium, dementia, and eventually death. Individuals typically present with the disease in adulthood, and the time course from onset of symptoms to death is approximately 18 months. There is no known cure for this condition.

Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease

Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD) is another prion disease leading to the development of a “sponge-like” brain. Symptoms include cognitive changes progressing to dementia, memory loss, myoclonic jerks, problems with speech, and seizures.7 It has a variety of causes. In some cases, it is genetic and is known as familial CJD (fCJD). These individuals tend to be affected later in life. There are also sporadic cases (sCJD). Iatrogenic CJD is typically caused by insufficient sterilization of medical equipment.8 Unlike viruses and bacteria, prions are incredibly resilient to most common sterilization techniques, including most chemicals and extreme temperatures. Finally, vCJD describes variant CJD, which is thought to be a result of the ingestion of prions derived from cows through contaminated beef products. As might be expected, there does not appear to be a correlation between advanced age and vCJD, as there is for fCJD.

 

Other Slow Viruses

There are some viruses described in other sections that are frequently categorizes as slow viruses. For example, AIDS is often described as a slow virus because of its very long incubation time and its tendency to progress to death. HTLV-1 and HTLV-2 are two retroviruses that have been associated with types of cancers that typically do not appear until decades after the original infection. Thus these too are also considered to be slow viruses in some contexts. Finally, some consider rabies (the rhabdovirus) to be a slow virus because of its long latency. However, the latency of rabies, which is on the scale of weeks to months, is often far shorter than the latency of the other slow viruses described previously.
