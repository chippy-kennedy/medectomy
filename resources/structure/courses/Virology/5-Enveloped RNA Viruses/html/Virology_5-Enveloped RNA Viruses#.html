Outer Structure

            Enveloped RNA viruses consist of either an icosahedral or helical protein capsid surrounded by a lipid-bilayer envelope.  Embedded within the envelope is an assortment of proteins and glycoproteins which aid in viral attachment and survival.1 Like enveloped DNA viruses, most enveloped RNA viruses obtain their lipid bilayer during a “budding” exit process from host cells.  This process aids enveloped RNA viruses in evading immune surveillance, and emphasizes the importance of cell-mediated immunity in fighting their infections.2

            Unlike naked viruses, the lipid bi-layer of enveloped RNA viruses makes them sensitive to detergents and drying.2  As a result, enveloped RNA viruses have decreased survival time on fomites and within the GI tract.  Overall, this makes them more likely to be transmitted via direct contact or respiratory droplets and less likely to be transmitted via the fecal-oral route.3  The exception to this rule is the Coronavirus Family, which is less sensitive to detergents and drying due to the presence of a unique “corona” of glycoproteins within its viral envelope.4   

Genome

            There are several factors which differentiate the genomes of enveloped RNA viruses.  Firstly, all enveloped RNA viruses have single-stranded RNA genomes.1  In addition to this, enveloped RNA viruses can either have segmented or non-segmented genomes.  The enveloped RNA viral families which consist of segmented genomes are as follows: Bunyavirus Family, Orthomyxovirus Family, and Arenavirus Family.5,6  Of these viruses, the Bunyaviruses and the Arenaviruses have circular, segmented genomes--whereas the Orthomyxoviruses have linear, segmented genomes.  The remainder of the enveloped RNA viruses have linear, non-segmented genomes. 

            As with RNA viruses in general, enveloped RNA viruses can consist of either a positive sense or a negative sense genome.  RNA viruses with a positive sense genome are coded in the 5’-3’ direction (identical in orientation to human mRNA) and can be easily translated by host ribosomes upon host-cell infiltration.  Due to this, positive sense RNA genomes are often referred to as “infectious” because insertion of the viral genetic material itself can lead to viral replication. 

            RNA viruses with a negative sense genome are coded in the 3’-5’ direction, and must be transcribed into a 5’-3’ positive sense template prior to viral protein translation.  This task is accomplished through the use of a viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase which must be carried within the nucleocapsid of negative sense RNA viruses.  By and large, all negative sense RNA viruses are enveloped and helical.

            Despite the differences in their individual replication mechanisms, all RNA viruses replicate in the host cell’s cytoplasm7

Enveloped RNA Viral Families

            Before delving into individual diseases, it is best to understand the hierarchy of viral families which fit into the category of enveloped RNA viruses.  One of the simplest ways to organize these viral families is by looking at the structural and genome characteristics which were discussed previously.  For example, an easy way to initially divide the enveloped RNA viruses is by whether they have a positive sense or negative sense RNA genome. 

            Following this format, the viral families which are enveloped and contain negative sense RNA genomes are as follows: Arenavirus Family, Bunyavirus Family, Paramyxovirus Family, Orthomyxovirus Family, Filovirus Family, Rhabdovirus Family.  These viruses can be remembered using the pneumonic “ Always brings polymerase or fails replication.” This pneumonic is relevant to the normal functioning of negative sense viruses, since they must carry an RNA-dependent RNA polymerase with them into the cell in order to replicate.  The negative sense, enveloped RNA viruses can be divided further based upon genome segmentation.  As stated earlier, the Bunyavirus Family, Orthomyxovirus Family, and Arenavirus Family have segmented genomes.  The remainder of the negative sense, enveloped RNA viruses have non-segmented, linear genomes. 

            The rest of the enveloped, RNA viruses contain non-segmented, positive sense genomes.  These viral families are as follows: Coronarvirus Family, Flavivirus Family, Togavirus Family, Retrovirus Family. 

            Overall, it is important to understand the structural and genomic divisions of the enveloped, RNA viruses; as both factors play an important role in defining the replication and pathophysiology of any given virus.  The remainder of this review chapter will be organized according to this hierarchy--first starting with the negative sense, enveloped RNA viruses and then describing the positive sense, enveloped RNA viruses. 

Negative Sense, Enveloped RNA Viruses

1.        The Arenavirus Family

The members of the Arenavirus family contain predominantly negative sense genomes.  However, interspersed within the genomes of Arenaviruses are positive sense segments.  Due to this, they are referred to as having an ambisense genome.  In addition to being ambisense, the Arenavirus genome also consists of two segments and is circular.  Another unique structural feature of Arenaviruses is the presence of small granules (which look like “grains of sand”) within each virus’s envelope.  These granules are thought to be host cell ribosomes which are incidentally picked up during the replication process.8

Nearly all of the Arenaviruses which infect humans are carried by rodent vectors.  Humans can contract Arenaviruses by direct contact with infected rodents or with contact with rodent droppings.  Due to this, Arenaviruses have a higher presence in rodent infested areas, such as slums.6  Although Arenaviruses are more common in areas such as Africa, they can also be contracted in poorer areas within the developed world. 

The common Arenaviruses which infect humans are the Lassa Fever Virus and the Lymphocytic Choriomeningitis Virus (LCMV).  Upon entering the body, both viruses establish a widespread viremia.  In the case of LCMV, viral infection usually leads to a flu-like illness which carries the possibility of progressing to meningitis or encephalitis.  Lethal outcome in cases of LCMV infection are rare, however.  The Lassa Fever virus, on the other hand, causes a hemmorhagic fever affecting nearly all internal organs.  Infection with this virus has a very high level of mortality.9  

2.        The Bunyavirus Family

The majority of the Bunyaviruses are Arboviruses--meaning that they are transmitted to humans via an arthropod vector.  The Arboviral Bunyaviruses include the following: California Encephalitis Virus, La Crosse Virus, Crimean-Congo Hemorrhagic Fever Virus, and Rift Valley Fever Virus.  These viruses are discussed in more detail within Chapter 27: Arboviruses.  Despite their transmission vector, however, all of the Bunyaviruses contain circular, negative-sense genomes with three segments. 9

The only non-Arboviral Bunyavirus is the Hantavirus.  Like Arenaviruses, the primary vector for Hantavirus transmission is rodents.  Humans are most likely to be infected by Hantaviruses upon being exposed to the aerosolized particles of infected rodent feces or urine.  There are several different sub-types of Hantaviruses, and they are classified according to their resulting symptamology.  Overall, the two primary classes are the Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome and the Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome. 

The Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome is caused by the Sin Nombre Virus which is endemic to the United States and Canada.  It is characterized by rapidly progressing pulmonary edema which results in a 60% mortality rate.   

The Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome is caused by the Hantaan Virus which is endemic to Asia and Southeastern Europe.  It results in a flu-like syndrome (possibly with a hemorrhagic component) which is followed by renal dysfunction.10

3.        The Paramyxovirus Family

Members of the Paramyxovirus Family are the causes of several important human diseases.  All of the important human Paramyxovirus pathogens cause some form of respiratory symptoms.  In addition, all of the Paramyxoviruses contain linear, non-segmented, negative-sense genomes.  Another feature which is common to all Paramyxovirus infections is the formation of multinucleated giant cells (synctitia) due to the presence of the viral envelope F protein.  The antibody Palivizumab was formulated to bind F protein and neutralize the function of Paramyxoviruses.11

Two of the Paramyxoviruses, the Parainfluenza and Respiratory Synctial Virus (RSV), tend to cause severe respiratory infections in infants and children.  By and large, the Parainfluenza Virus is the primary cause of croup in children.  Croup infections are characterized by the presence of a “seal-like, barking cough.”  RSV, on the other hand, is the primary cause of bronchiolitis (inflammation of the bronchioles) in infants and children.  It is commonly treated with the antiviral Ribavirin.12

Another one of the common Paramyxoviruses is the Rubeola Virus, which is the cause of measles in humans.  Measles is most likely to affect unvaccinated children, and is characterized by the 3 C’s: Cough, Coryza, and Conjunctivitis.  It also causes the development of diagnostic red spots with a blueish center on the buccal mucosa referred to as Koplik Spots.  In most cases the Rubeola virus also causes the development of a rash which spreads from head to toe.  Possible sequelae of measles infection include the development a giant cell pneumonia or a subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE).  SSPE occurs years after a measles infection, and is due to the persistence of the Rubeloa virus in glial cells and neurons--although it is a rare condition.  The best protection for patients from the Rubeola virus is in the form of a live-attenuated vaccine which is administered during childhood as a part of the MMR vaccination series.13 

The final Paramyxovirus which causes a major disease in humans is the Mumps Virus.  Overall, the Mumps Virus causes the development of Parotitis (swelling of the parotid gland), Orchitis (swelling of the testes), and aseptic meningitis in infected individuals.  A possible sequelae is the development of sterility in male patients.  As with the Rubeola Virus, the best protection for patients from the Mumps Virus is the administration of a live-attenuated vaccine as  apart of the MMR series.14

 

 

4.        The Orthomyxovirus Family

 The members of the Orthomyxovirus Family represent important human pathogens: the Influenza Viruses.  All of the members of this viral category contain negative-sense, single-stranded, segmented genomes with eight segments.  This makes the virus highly variable, with the swapping of viral segments leading to epidemic-causing antigenic shift. Unlike the other RNA viruses, the Orthomyxoviruses replicate and assemble in the host cell nucleus as opposed to the cytosol.  In addition, they also steal host cell 5’ caps in order to promote viral genome translation.15 

The Influenza Viruses contain two important envelope proteins: neuraminidase and hemagglutinin.  The main function of hemagglutinin is to promote viral entry into host cells, and the main function of neuraminidase is to promote the release of viral particles following replication.  Two antivirals, Zanamivir (Relenza) and Oseltamivir (Tamiflu), act as inhibitors of neuraminidase.

Within humans, the Influenza Virus causes an acute respiratory tract infection which is characterized by sudden onset and high fever.  Overall, the Influenza Virus has more cataclysmic consequences in very young and elderly patients.  However, primary infection with Influenza can lead to dysfunction of the muco-ciliary escalator and predisposition to the development of lethal bacterial pneumonias. 

The best prevention of the influenza virus is the widespread usage of a seasonal vaccine.  Two major forms of this quadrivalent vaccine exist--including an inactivated intramuscular vaccine and a live-attenuated intranasal vaccine.16

5.        The Filovirus Family

The members of the Filovirus Family possess single stranded, non-segmented, negative-sense, RNA genomes.  They are also characterized by a highly filamentous, helical nucleocapsid.  Not much is known about the vector of the Filoviruses, but the African Green Monkey is a possible candidate. 

The two major members of the Filovirus Family are the Marburg and Ebola Viruses.  Both of them are the cause of a major, rapidly-progressing, hemorrhagic fever in humans.  Outbreaks of Filovirus infections generally only occur in Africa, and they are actually self-limited by their high lethality (greater than 90%) and rapid progression.  Entire populations are destroyed before the virus has the opportunity to spread to a neighboring village. Due to their lethality, Filoviruses are a possible mechanism of bio-terrorism.17  

6.        The Rhabdovirus Family

The members of the Rhabdovirus Family contain single-stranded, non-segmented, negative-sense, RNA genomes.  Upon examination with electron microscopy, Rhabdoviruses are characterized by a “bullet-shaped” envelope and capsid.6  

The primary member of the Rhabdovirus family which affects humans in the Rabies Virus.  There are several clinical and pathological characteristics which are diagnostic for the Rabies Virus.  First of all, the Rabies Virus is almost always transmitted to humans via a bite-wound from an infected animal.  Bats are a common vector of Rabies.  In addition to animal bites, cave-explorers are also at risk of contracting the Rabies Virus through exposure to the aerosolized droppings of cave-dwelling bats. 

Once infected with Rabies, the virus enters host cells via fusion with the Nicotinic Ach receptor at the neuromuscular junction.  After entering neurons, the Rabies Virus travels up host axons via retrograde transport until it enters the CNS.  This process accounts for the long incubation period (weeks to months) of Rabies Virus infection.  It also allows for the successful vaccination after infection by the virus. 

At first, Rabies infection leads to the development of malaise and general flulike symptoms.  Over the course of weeks, these symptoms progress into strange neurological manifestations such as photophobia, hydrophobia, and hyper-agitation.  The infection culminates in the development of coma and death.  Following the development of outward symptoms of infection, there are no reliable forms of clinical treatment. 

Upon autopsy or biopsy, the neurons of infected individuals have characteristic cytoplasmic inclusions known as Negri Bodies.

As stated previously, the best treatment of the Rabies Virus is post-infection vaccination with an inactivated vaccine.  This allows the production of an effective patient immune response to the Rabies virus before CNS infilatration.  Post-exposure treatment also includes infusion of human recombinant rabies Ig.  Some clinicians have begun to treat symptomatic rabies with medically induced coma--giving time for a patient immune response to develop--yielding mixed results.  Prevention is carried out by the vaccination of wild and domestic animals.18 

Positive-Sense Enveloped RNA Viruses

1.        The Coronavirus Family

The members of the Coronavirus Family contain single-stranded, non-segmented, positive-sense, RNA genomes.  Although members of the Coronavirus Family are enveloped with a lipid-bilayer, they are less sensitive to drying, detergents, and low pH’s than the majority of enveloped viruses.  This is due to the presence of a closely-packed glycoprotein “corona” within the Coronavirus’s envelope, which confers characteristics similar to those of naked viruses.4 

For the most part, the majority of Coronaviruses cause the “common cold” in humans.  This is a disease which is hallmarked by upper respiratory symptoms (rhinorrhea, sneezing, post-nasal drip) with lack of significant fever or lower-respiratory involvement.  However, in the fall of 2002, there was an outbreak caused by a mutated strain of Coronavirus which caused a fatal lower-respiratory tract infection.  This disease was code-named SARS (severe, acute, respiratory distress syndrome) and its causal Coronavirus deemed the SARS Virus.  There have been no major outbreaks of the SARS Virus since.19

2.        Flaviviruses

Members of the Flavivirus Family contain single-stranded, non-segmented, positive-sense, RNA genomes.  The majority of the members of the Flavivirus Family are Arboviruses--meaning that they are transmitted by arthropod vectors.  These Flavivirial Arboviruses include the following: Yellow Fever Virus, Dengue Fever Virus, West Nile Virus, St. Louis Encephalitis Virus, and Japanese Encephalitis Virus.  These pathogens are discussed in greater detail in Chapter 27: Arboviruses.  In addition to the Arboviruses, the Flavivirus Family also includes the Hepatitis C Virus.20 

Hepatitis C Virus is very similar to Hepatitis B Virus in terms of course and severity.  Like HBV, the Hepatitis C Virus is primarily transferred via bodily fluids.  Unlike HBV, however, HCV is more likely to be transferred via direct blood-blood contact than sexual contact.  Therefore, blood transfusions are a major route of HCV transmission.  Like HBV, HCV has the ability to cause chronic infections of the liver which can lead to cirrhosis and increased risk for hepatocellular carcinoma.21 

3.        Togavirus Family

The members of the Togavirus Family contain single-stranded, non-segmented, positive-sense, RNA genomes.  As with the Flavivirus Family, the Togavirus Family contains several prominent Arboviruses.  These viruses are primarily transmitted by arthropod vectors, and they include the Western Equine Encephalitis Virus, Eastern Equine Encephalitis Virus, and Venezuelan Equine Encephalitis Virus.  All of these viruses are discussed in more detail within Chapter 27: Arboviruses.6 

In addition to the Togaviral Arboviruses, the Togavirus Family contains another important human pathogen: the Rubella Virus.  The disease caused by the Rubella Virus in humans is often referred to as “German Measles” or “Three Day Measles.”  It is characterized by fevers, adenopathy, joint pain, and a rash which mainly affects the trunk.  In many ways, the truncal rash caused by Rubella is similar to the rash which eventually manifests as a part of infection by Rubeola Virus (measles).  For the most part, infection by the Rubella Virus is self-limiting, and resolves within a week.

The main morbidity caused by the Rubella Virus results from infection during pregnancy.  During pregnancy the Rubella Virus can readily cross the placenta--resulting in disruption of normal embryonic growth, especially during the first trimester.  This growth disruption results in a multitude of severe birth defects, such as microcephaly, cataracts, deafness, and cardiac defects.  Due to this, it is standard practice to measure Rubella antibody IgM levels during the first three months of pregnancy. 

The main prevention of the Rubella virus is the administration of a live-attenuated vaccine during childhood, as a part of the MMR vaccine series.  Successful vaccination programs have led to a significant decrease in Rubella-based birth defects, and women who have not received the MMR in childhood should be offered the vaccination before attempting to become pregnant.20 

4.        The Retrovirus Family

The Retrovirus Family contain single-stranded, non-segmented, positive-sense RNA genomes.  Overall, the Retroviruses represent an important group of human pathogens.  In addition, although they are classified as “positive-sense,” they undergo a special form of viral replication which results in the formation of dsDNA which is subsequently inserted within the host genome.  The two main retroviruses which affect humans are HTLV-1 and Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV). 

Within its capsid, each retrovirus contains several elements which are essential to its replication.  These include two strands of positive-sense RNA, two tRNAs, an integrase, and a reverse transcriptase.  Following host cell-infiltration, the replication elements are released into the cell’s cytoplasm and the reverse transcriptase binds the viral RNA.  Using the tRNA as a primer, the reverse transcriptase synthesizes a complementary, negative-sense strand of DNA. Following this, the reverse transcriptase degrades the RNA genome and synthesizes the positive-sense strand of DNA.  This double-stranded DNA is then translocated to the nucleus, and integrated into the host genome via the use of the viral integrase.22 

Within the retroviral dsDNA are several genes which are essential to viral replication and survival.  Three of the genes which are essential to viral replication are tat, rev, and the LTR sequence.  In addition, there are three genes which are essential to the formation of viral structural elements.  These are gag, pol, and env.  The pol gene encodes the reverse transcriptase and integrase.  The env gene encodes the retroviral envelope glycoproteins--gp120 and gp41.  Overall, gp120 is essential to retroviral attachment to host cells, and gp41 is essential to retroviral fusion and entry.  In addition to the structural and replication genes, the viral genes vif and nef act to increase the infectivity of retroviruses.  Finally, the vpr, vpx, and vpu genes control the rate of replication and release of retroviruses.23  

Like most retroviruses, HTLV-1 is transmitted via contact with infected blood and body fluids.  The primary form of transmission is sexual contact.  Within humans, HTLV-1 infects CD4+ T-cells.  Once inserted within the host genome, the HTLV-1 DNA causes the accidental transactivation of host oncogenes--leading to the uncontrolled proliferation of host cells.  Therefore, HTLV-1 infection can lead to the development of adult acute T-cell lymphocytic leukemia (ATLL).  The malignant cells in HTLV-1 infection have been termed “flower cells,” and the presence of these cells along with skin lesions similar to those found in Sezary Syndrome are clinically diagnostic.  Overall, HTLV-1 infections are endemic to Japan.22  

Like HTLV-1, HIV is transmitted via blood-blood and sexual contact.  The virus enters host CD4+ T cells by binding the CXCR4 and CD4 receptors, and enters host macrophages by binding the CCR5 and CD4 receptors.  Although CD4+ T cells are the primary cell-type affected by HIV infection, infiltration of host macrophages is necessary for the establishment of a progressive infection.  Due to this, a heterozygous CCR5 mutation in the host leads to a significantly slower course of infection, and a homozygous CCR5 mutation leads to immunity from infection.22  

Overall, HIV infection in humans is characterized by the initial development of flu-like symptoms.  Then, the viral infection will remain asymptomatic for a period of months to years, while the number of circulating host CD4+ T cells continues to drop.  Eventually, an acquired state of immunodeficiency develops in the host as a result of the infection, leading to the development of susceptibility to other infections.  When the host CD4+ T cell count drops below 200, this state is defined as Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS).  Patients with AIDS are predisposed to a number of unusual infections, including a lethal interstitial pneumonia caused by Pneumocytis jirovecii.24

Although research into an HIV vaccine is ongoing, the best current form of HIV prevention includes safe sex, transfusion screening, and sharps safety.  Diagnosis of HIV infection is normally done with an ELISA, and confirmed with Western Blot.  However, ELISA can be falsely negative during the first few months of infection, leaving RT-PCR as the only true form of early detection.25 

Several forms of HIV antivirals exist, all of which target retroviral-specific enzymes.  The classes of HIV antivirals include: nucleoside-based reverse transcriptase inhibitors, non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors, protease inhibitors, binding/fusion inhibitors, and integrase inhibitors.  The current standard of HIV care is the use of Highly Active Antiretroviral Therapy (HAART), which includes the simultaneous use of three different antiretrovirals.  Most forms of HAART contain two different types of nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors paired with either one non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor or one fusion inhibitor.26
