Overview

Actinomycetes are a group of Gram-positive bacterial rods that morphologically resemble fungal hyphae because they’re are elongated and branch into long filaments. Actinomycetes include 2 medically relevant bacteria: (1) Actinomyces israelii and (2) Nocardia asteroides. Actinomyces is anerobic while Nocardia asteroides is aerobic [see figure 1]. Although classically grouped with other Gram-positive bacteria, Nocardia asteroides is weakly acid-fast due to the presence of mycolic acids in the cell wall.

Actinomyces

Overview

Actinomyces is a genus of Gram-positive rod-shaped bacteria that causes the disease called actinomycosis. The genus is almost entirely anaerobic. Actinomyces do not produce endospores, and are not acid-fast. Clinically and in culture, Actinomyces forms thin, hyphae-like filaments that resemble the growth patterns of fungi (Actinomyces is etymologically derived from the Greek "mykes", meaning fungus)2.

Epidemiology, Transmission & Pathogenesis

Actinomyces infection is more common in rural areas and in twice as common in men than in women. Actinomyces typically does not spread between persons, although human bites can cause infection with Actinomyces. Instead, infections are typically "endogenous", meaning the infection enters the body from a foreign source such as ground soil or contaminated water. Actinomyces is part of the normal flora, a normal colonizer of the mouth and gums, GI tract, and female GU tract. Actinomyces is not highly virulent, and infections are typically preceded by disruption of the protective mucosal layers in these tissues and injury that leads to formation of an anaerobic environment2.

A common etiology of Actinomyces infection is poor oral hygiene; the bacteria may invade through oral abscesses, or following dental surgeries and traumatic injuries to the face or mouth.2,3

Actinomyces species may also cause thoracic infections following aspiration of the bacteria from the oropharynx. Infections of the gut, abdomen, and central nervous system are also possible. Abdominal infections are most frequently secondary to GI infections (except in cases of female patients caused by intrauterine devices), while infections of the CNS are usually mediated by transport of bacteria in the blood2,3.

Disease & Clinical Presentation

The classic disease manifestation of Actinomyces is called actinomycosis. Actinomycosis features lesions that eventually fill with pus with yellow "sulfur-like granules" to form abscesses. The yellow "sulfur-like granules" is due to filaments of Actinomyces bacteria becoming bound together by calcium phosphate to take on a yellowish, sand-like color. These colonies are visible macroscopically, and are frequently found in the sinus tracts and lesions2,3. The pus is formed by dead neutrophils.

Actinomycosis is unique from other infectious diseases because Actinomyces gradually invades tissues by direct invasion and infiltration. This movement is facilitated by "draining sinus tracts", tiny channels through the tissues that connect the pockets of infectious tissue. One most take care to differentiate "sinus tracts" from the sinuses in the skull - in cervicofacial actinomycosis, the bacteria are actually "carving" their own sinus tracts through the infected bone2.

The following are specific infections of actinomycosis:

(1) Cervicofacial infection, the most common manifestation of actinomycoses. Cervicofacial actinomycosis is characterized by tissue swelling in the region of the neck and face (especially the jaw), with fibrosis and scarring in the affected areas. Sinus tracts draining the swollen areas are present, and typically follow the jaw line2,3.

(2) Pneumonia & Thoracic infection. Thoracis actinomycosis is characterized by abscesses in lung tissue, typically caused by aspiration into areas of the lungs with decreased oxygen levels (atelectasis) in patients with poor dental hygiene. These abscesses may spread through adjacent tissues, forming a draining sinus through the chest wall2. These patients present with cough, fever, pleural effusion, and sinus tracts.

(3) Abdominal infection. Abdominal actinomycosis can spread to every organ inside the abdominal cavity2.

(4) Pelvic inflammatory disease. Pelvic actinomycosis causes 1-4% of pelvic inflammatory disease. Women with intra-uterine devices are at risk. Tubo-ovarian abscesses are common. Infrequently, the only manifestation is mild vaginitis2,4.

(5) Central Nervous System. CNS actinomycosis most commonly takes the form of individual brain abscesses. Meningitis and empyemas of the meninges are also possible2.

Diagnosis

Microscopically, Actinomyces will appear as yellow sulfur-like granules surrounded by neutrophils. Actinomyces is a slow-growing, fastidious bacteria. Colonies grown in culture are white in color with an irregular morphology reminiscent of the top surface of a tooth. Biochemical testing can differentiate individual Actinomyces species, but identifying the genus is typically sufficient2.

Treatment & Prevention

Actinomycosis is treated with surgical drainage and debridement of abscesses and infected tissues, in combination with long-term antibiotic treatment. Actinomyces is susceptible to penicillin, carbapenems, macrolides, and clindamycin, with penicillin G being the drug of choice. If antibiotics do not resolve the symptoms, it is likely another abscess is present somewhere in the body1,2.

Being able to identify the bacteria is important as diagnosis will guide treatment and management of bacterial infections. Most bacteria can be divided into 2 broad categories: Gram positive (+) and Gram negative (-) bacteria. Performing a gram stain makes this basic distinction. This lab test reveals the cell wall morphology of bacteria, as well as helps visualize the shape of the colorless bugs[1].

 

Gram Stain:

Gram Stain: Helps you distinguish between gram positive and gram-negative bacteria. Purple stains indicate a positive result. Pink stains indicate the presence of gram-negative bacteria. A gram-positive bacteria is not able to be decolorized by the alcohol due to the presence of a thick layer of peptidoglycan and therefore is unaffected by the counter-stain. A Gram-negative bacterium, due to its thinner peptidoglycan cell wall, decolorizes and becomes stained by the safranin dye upon application[2]. The steps for performing a gram stain in the laboratory are as follows:

a.       Obtain a glass slide and place a drop of water on it.

b.      Sterilize an inoculation loop and transfer bacteria from a culture onto the drop of water and mix until a faint cloudiness appears.

c.       Heat fix the slide.

d.      Add crystal violet dye for 1 minute. Rinse with tap water.

e.       Add iodine for 1 minute. Rinse with tap water. This step causes precipitation of the primary stain in the peptidoglycan cell wall.

f.       Add Acetone/Alcohol to decolorize for 2-3 second. Rinse with tap water. The acetone serves to dissolve the outer membrane of bacteria and wash out the crystal violet from a thin peptidoglycan wall, but not from the thick cell wall.

g.      Apply safranin, counterstain, for 30 seconds. Rinse with tap water. This will be taken up by gram-negative bacteria that have just lost their thin outer wall.

h.      Drain and dry. Place slide under microscope[2].

 

While the gram stain can successfully identify most bacteria, there remain a few types that are resistant to the test or cause variability in results. This can occur if there is a modified cell wall or if the bacterial cultures have been treated with beta-lactam antibiotics, which weaken the peptidoglycan wall, decreasing its dye retention[1]. Gram resistant organisms include Mycobacterium, Nocardia, Mycoplasma, Ricketssia, Coxiella, Chlamydia, Treponema, and Borrelia. Other staining techniques, which will be discussed, must be implemented to identify these bacteria[1].

One of the most important tests utilized for lab diagnosis of tuberculosis is the Ziehl-Neelson stain, also known as the acid-fast stain[1]. Mycobacterium and Nocardia are two bugs that contain a waxy outer layer made of mycolic acid, which is resistant to gram staining. The acid-fast stain, outlined below, is the preferred method for testing[1]. A positive result would result in red stained cells, because the mycolic acid in the cell wall will not dissolve upon contact with acid alcohol, and will stay “color fast,” hence the moniker[4].

 

Acid-Fast

Acid-fast stain: The acid fast stain helps detect bacteria containing mycolic acid in its cell wall. Red indicates the organism is acid fast (Mycobacterium or Nocardia) whereas blue is a negative result. The steps to do an acid-fast stain are as follows[3].

a. A smear of sputum or culture is applied to a testing slide. Air dry and heat fix.

b. A red stain called Carbolfushsin is applied to the smear and heated to allow proper absorption into the sample. Allow to sit for a couple minutes, and then rinse with tap water.

c. Pour a mixture of acid alcohol (95% ethanol, 3% HCl) onto the smear for 1 min. Then rinse.

d. Apply methylene-blue counterstain for 1 minute. Rinse.

e. Blot the sample dry and view under a microscope with an oil immersion lens[4].

The other gram-resistant organisms listed previously are not as easily identified with staining techniques. Mycoplasma is unique because it is the only bacteria lacking a cell wall. It requires sterols for growth and is identified through antigen specific IgM antibody tests[3]. Rickettsia, Coxiella, and Chlamydia are obligate intracellular bacteria. Treponema, the organism responsible for syphilis, can only be seen through dark field microscopy and fluorescent antibody stains. Borrelia, a spirochete organism, can be visualized through a Giemsa stain[1].

So far, the staining methods used to differentiate basic bacterial structure and shape have been discussed. These techniques have been in use for a long time, but they can have a high degree of variability due to human error, improper staining techniques, and poor sampling of bacteria. Medium-based tests are easier, more accurate ways to differentiate metabolic properties of bacteria after basic gram classification has been made[1]. A majority of bacteria are compatible with growth on a non-selective blood agar media, which contains general nutrients and 5% sheep blood; this can be used to isolate bacterial colonies and observe colony morphology[1]. The differentiation of the Streptococcus species is of special importance in regards to growth on blood agar plates[5]. Certain strains of Streptococcus exhibit varying growth patterns on the blood agar, and these can be used to differentiate them more easily. For example, both Group A and Group B Streptococcus exhibit beta hemolysis of the blood agar plate; they completely lyse red blood cells (RBCs) surrounding their colonies through streptolysin O and S enzymes. Staphylococcus aureus is also the only beta-hemolytic bacteria of its species. Other Streptococcus species such as S. pneumoniae and S. viridans exhibit alpha-hemolysis, or partial clearing of the blood agar plate; this results in a greenish color around the colonies due to the presence of biliverdin, a breakdown product of hemoglobin[5]. Gamma-hemolysis (no clearing of RBCs) occurs with Enterococcus sp. and Group D Streptococcus. These characteristics are very useful in initial classification of this large gram (+) bacteria species; selective media, biochemical testing, and antibiotic testing separate them even further[5].

Selective mediums work by inhibiting growth of some bacteria, while differential mediums include an identifying test[1]. Special mediums contain certain metabolites needed for growth of specific bacteria. Figure 1 outlines the various types of differential media plates used to colonize different bacteria[1]. Figure 2 outlines the special medias commonly used. The various biochemical tests commonly utilized in further classification are outlined below.

 

Biochemical Tests

1.      Catalase Test: Staphylococcus vs. Streptococcus.

a.       Both Staphylococcus and Streptococcus are gram positive organisms and appear as cocci (round ovals) on a gram stain. The catalase test, used to differentiate between the two bacterial cocci, detects the presence of the catalase enzyme commonly found in the cytochrome of most bacteria, except Streptococcus[2]. The procedure is as follows:

                                                        i.            Take a group of 5-10 bacterial colonies with a sterile loop and place it on a clean glass slide. Smear the colonies and then put a few drops of 3% H2O2. The presence of bubbling is a positive test, which is indicative of Staphylococcus. A lack of reactivity identifies the colony as Streptococcus[2].

2.      Latex Coagulase Test: Staphylococcus aureus vs. other Staphylococcus species[2]

a.       Staph organisms are all catalase positive. This test helps distinguish between S.aureus and the other staph species by detecting the presence of coagulase enzymes[2]. The testing procedure is as follows:

                                                        i.            Put a drop of latex reagent which has been coated with human fibrinogen and IgG on a black cardboard slide. With an application stick, place 6 colonies in the reagent in order to make an even, clump free mixture. Title the slide for 20 seconds. If white clumps appear, it means the reaction is positive and that the species is S.aureus[2].

3.      Oxidase Test: Distinguishes between gram-negative species.

a.       Take a filter paper and place a drop of oxidase reagent on it. Take a colony from an agar plate and put on the oxidase reagent. If a purple-blue dot appears the reaction is positive[2].

4.      Urease test: This test is trying to detect the presence of the urease[2], an enzyme that breaks down urea into carbon dioxide and ammonia[5]. The mixture is prepared by adding urea to a broth, as well as some nutrients to facilitate growth and phenol red as an indicator. In an acidic environment, the broth turns yellow[5]. In an alkaline environment with a pH of 7.2-8.8, the solution will pink, indicating a rapid urease-positive test with the presence of Proteus species[2].

a.       Take the prepared urease broth and add a sample of a colony with a sterile bacteria loop. Wait for 5 minutes and check for color change.

 

In addition to the biochemical tests listed above, antibiotic sensitivity testing plays a key role in differentiating bacteria of the same species, especially the gram (+) Streptococcus species. Two antibiotics to be aware of are bacitracin and optochin. Bacitracin (also called A disc in the laboratory) differentiates Group A Streptococcus pyogenes from other Streptococcal species; only Group A S. pyogenes is sensitive[4]. Optochin (also called P disc in the laboratory) differentiates between alpha-hemolytic S. pneumoniae and S. viridans. S. pneumoniae is optochin-sensitive, while S. viridans is optochin-resistant[4].

These various methods of bacterial classification are widely used in laboratories across the world, and are immensely useful for diagnosis of infectious disease. Proper knowledge of specific properties of bacteria species is especially useful when presented with laboratory data.

Blood Protozoa

Overview

Protozoa are single celled eukaryotic cells complete with organelles (3). They have two layers of cytoplasm (ectoplasm and endoplasm) and can range from 5 µm to 2 mm. They ingest food through their cytosome. They can reproduce asexually through division or sexually through a process which requires fusion, followed by an exchange of DNA and a separation. Many protozoa transition between an inactive cyst form and an active trophozoite form. (4) This transition is often provoked by changes in the protozoan’s environment, which serves as a trigger for the next stage of the life cycle. Parasites of the blood are generally transmitted via an arthropod vector, but can be transmitted via blood (1). Tissue parasites, on the other hand, can be transmitted via food, contact with mucus membranes, or an artheropod vector like the blood protozoa.

Like the intestinal protozoa, detection is often possible through PCR, examination of blood or tissue, and occasionally staining techniques. Symptoms are generally unique to the pathogen. Treatment is generally done through anti-protozoa medications. Prevention methods depend on the method of infection, but generally revolve around prophylactic medications, elimination of the arthropod vector or the ability of the arthropod to infect, proper handling of meat, or avoiding endemic areas.

There are 3 major categories of medically-relevant protozoa: (1) blood and tissue protozoa (2) intestinal protozoa and (3) urogenital protozoa. This chapter will discuss the blood and tissue protozoa. Within this group, there are 3 major categories: (1) amebas (2) flagellates and (3) sporozoans.

Within amebas, there are 2 species: (1) Naegleri folweri and (2) Acanthamoeba.

Flagellates: (1) Leishmania (2) Trypanosoma

Sporozoans: (1) Plasmodium (2) Toxoplasma gondii (3) Babesia.

Sporozoans

Plasmodium

The genus of Plasmodium is best known for causing malaria. There are 5 major species of Plasmodium associated with malaria in humans: (1) P. falciparum, (2) P. knowlesi, (3) P vivax, (4) P. ovale, and (5) P. malariae.

Epidemiology

Malaria is responsible for 1-5 billion febrile episodes and 1-3 million deaths every year, 85% of which are in Africa (1). Malaria has been eradicated from the United States, Canada and Europe and most malarial infections are now found in tropical areas. P. falciparum is typically found in Africa and Haiti while P. vivax is more common in Central America. Although the disease can be dangerous to anyone infected, children under 5 are the most vulnerable.

Clinical Presentation of Malaria

There are certain diseases that provide an evolutionary advantage by protecting against malaria. These conditions include (1) sickle cell trait and sickle cell disease (2) thalassemias, (3) G6PD deficiency and (4) ovalocytosis (an inherited condition where RBCs have an oval shape). Many of these conditions are prevalent in areas endemic to malaria because of the increased evolutionary survival benefit. Similarly, patients who do not have Duffy blood group A and B expressed on the RBCs are protected against P. vivax infection.

Transmission of Plasmodium

All 5 major species are spread by the female Anopheles mosquito. While most cases of malaria in the United States are due to persons who visited or lived in an endemic area, it is possible for the infection to be spread via blood transfusions, contaminated needle, or congenitally via transplacental transmission, though all of these methods of transmission are relatively rare (1).

Life Cycle of Plasmodium

Anopheles mosquito spreads infectious sporozoites via its saliva (1). These sporozoites are then carried to the parenchymal cells of the liver, where they implant and asexually reproduce. Since they exist both in the red blood cells and outside of them, this portion of the cycle is the exoerythrocytic cycle. P. vivax and P. ovale create dormant hepatic phases with no division (hypnozoites). As the sporozoites mature, they eventually become merozoites, which rupture from the liver cells and travel to the red blood cells, where they begin the erythrocytic cycle. This is important because dormant hepatic Plasmodium is not susceptible to the same medications as active Plasmodium, which means different treatment strategies must be employed when P. vivax and P. ovale are suspected, even in combined infections.

Within the erythrocytes, the parasites replicate through a ring stage, trophozoite stage, and schizont stage, eventually leading to a rupture of the red blood cell, spreading even more merozoties. Occasionally, the merozoites develop into male and female gametocytes. It is pairs of these gametocytes that are ingested by mosquitos in order to continue the endemic infection (1).

 

 

All malarial subtypes can present with dark urine and low hematocrit from increased RBC lysis (5).

P. falciparum

P. falciparum causes the most serious form of malaria, with majority of deaths from malaria being caused by P. falciparum infections due to complications from cerebral malaria and pulmonary edema. P. falciparum is found most often in Tropical and Subtropical areas, in areas where HIV is a threat too, thus co-infection may be likely depending on patient population (1). Most notably, like a number of other Plasmodium species, African counties are most strongly associated with this infection.

This species of Plasmodium is remarkable for its pathogenic potential and high parasite load. During any stage in its life cycle, it is capable of invading host erythrocytes, and multiple merozoites can infect one RBC, leading to rapidly progressive infections. Malaria caused by P. falciparum is called malignant malaria for this reason. The infection is so aggressive it can invade up to 30% of the host’s blood cells (4). An infection presents with a very short incubation period and flu-like symptoms that move to daily chills and fever, with potential for progressive nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Attacks may be cyclic, occurring every 2 days. There is potential to mimic other illnesses that present with GI symptoms (1). Severe, untreated infections can progress to clotting due to cellular debris, or even involvement of the brain. If the infection reaches the kidney, blackwater fever results, presenting with acute renal failure and hemoglobinuria (1)

Diagnosis of P. falciparum malaria

P. falciparum can be distinguished in a number of ways. Under light microscope in a peripheral blood smear, infected erythrocytes will have ring forms near the edge or stuck along the outside of the host cells and banana-shaped gametocytes within RBCs in thin blood smear. This presentation is called the appliqué position and is unique to infection with P. falciparum. A peripheral smear may also reveal mature crescent-shaped gametocytes or ring forms, and may host cells may contain Maurer dots¸ which are red granules also associated with this Sprozoa (1). Diagnosis can also occur through RDT antigen test using antibodies against the specific species based on enzymes these very sensitive and specific for P. falciparum, but do not work as well for other species.

Treatment & Prevention of P. falciparum malaria

Treatment with chloroquine is classical, but becoming more difficult as species develop resistance. Quinine via IV is a good idea. Mefloquine and artesunate is another option. Doxycycline is considered a potential treatment in cases where chloroquine is not viable. Severe malaria may necessitate red cell exchange. Even in cerebral infection, anticonvulsants are not recommended. Though P. falciparum cannot relapse, it is necessary to ensure that co-morbid conditions are also treated. Prevention occurs through chemoprophylaxis, control of the mosquito population, and prevention of mosquito bites, through sprays, netting, and protective clothing.

P. vivax

One of the largest differentiations between P. vivax and the rest of the Pasmodium is that it only invades young erythrocytes. This does this by infecting via the Duffy blood antigen. It is the most prevalent of the human plasmodia, covering South America, Southeast Asia, and a number of other tropical, subtropical, and temperate regions. As mentioned above, the life cycle of P. vivax involves a dormant hepatic stage, allowing for re-infection if the hepatic stage is not treated in addition to the erythrocytic stage. Incubation generally occurs over a 1-2 week period. The first symptoms are flu-like, consisting of fever, chills, fatigue, and perhaps a headache. As the infection progresses, a typical pattern of more severe chills, fever, and rigors presents, reappearing every 48 hours as the cycle of infection continues. The fevers can reach very high temperatures and may pose a danger to the patient. However, this malaria can be tolerated for years. More severe infections may develop severe organ disinfection, with chronic symptoms creating micro-blockages.

Thick blood films and thin films can reveal infection via microscope. Under light microscope, infected RBC’s possess pink granules called Schüffner dots. Can also contain the typical malaraial pigment called hemozoin, which is golden-brown in mature schizonts. RDT’s are effective but not as effective as those for other species.

Treat with Chloroquine to suppress this infection and manage the symptoms. Primaquine is effect as a radical cure and for elimination of the gametocytes. For this reason, treatment with primaquine is effective in preventing relapse from latent forms.

P. ovale

Like P. vivax, it is selective for young erythrocytes. This parasite is distributed throughout tropical Africa, with some infection in Asia and South America. Infections are similar to vivax, with attacks every 48 hours (tertian). However, infection typically only lasts for a year. Like vivax, replase occurs due to liver infiltration. It is called ovale because it distorts its host cells into the shape of an oval. Also like in vivax, it presents with Schüffner dots. Unlike vivax, its malarial pigment is darker and it contains far fewer merozoites in its mature schizont. Treat with primaquine to ensure that latent liver infection is dealt with. Treatment is similar in respects to vivax¸ including in prevention.

P. malariae

This species is less prevalent than some other subtypes but similar in prevalent locations. Of all the malarial parasites, this species has the longest incubation period, typically 17-40 days but up to months or years. The syndrome periods themselves occur in 72 hour intervals. Attacks are moderate to severe, but are not necessarily lethal and patients can last untreated for up to 20 years (1).

Unlike the other malarial parasites, these species can only infect mature erythrocytes. As a result, it grows smaller and does not distort the RBC’s. However, dark staining forms can still be seen, presenting as “band and bar forms”. Schizont presents with eight merozoites in a rosette. Under light microscope, Ziemann dots can be seen. Treatment for this species is similar to those mentioned above and must include treatment to prevent recurrence.

P. knowlesi

Like P. falciparum, P. knowlesi causes malaria. However, this species is a typical parasite with an animal reservoir, subsisting in “Old World” monkeys, namely those in Asia, such as Malaysia and other Southeast Asian countries. This species is transmitted by the Anopheles leucosyphrus mosquito. Like P. falciparum, infection of RBC’s can occur at any point during the life cycle. Unlike, P. falciparum, however, the life cycle is short. As a result, symptoms do not last nearly as long. Otherwise, it is very similar to other species, with the exception of not being associated with neurological symptoms. Additionally, it does not produce liver hypnosporozoites and thus will not lie dormant after primary treatment.

Symptoms present with daily fever, headaches, and chills, but can progress to tachypnea and tachycardia. Only 7% of illnesses progress to a “severe” state. Death if they occur often result from pulmonary or hepatorenal failure.

Diagnosis is performed by recognition of ring forms, multiple trophozoites, and double chromatin dots under light microscope. PCR is also a possibility, though less likely in the field. No RDT is available. Treatment is generally done with chloroquine. Prevention of mosquito bites is a must. The problem of an animal reservoir means that this species is difficult or impossible to eliminate

Babesia

In addition to Plasmodium, sporozoans from the genus Babesia can cause intraerythrocytic infection. Babesia microti cause the disease called babesiosis.

Epidemiology

Babesia microti is the most common species causing babesiosis in the United States. The disease is relatively rate with most reported cases of babesiosis primarily occurring in Northeastern United States (Connecticut and Cape Cod, Massachusetts). Babesiosis has been reported in other countries although it remains relatively rare.

Transmission & Life cycle of Babesia microti

Unlike Plasmodium which is spread by a mosquito, B. microti is transmitted by a tick called Ixodes scapularis (I. dammini). This tick also serves as a vector for Borrelia burgdorferi, the bacteria that cause Lyme disease (co-infections can occur in 15% of patients and can increase the severity of symptoms). Infections of parasites from the genus Babesia present as zoonosis with humans as accidental hosts. Primary hosts include cattle, deer, and rodents. Infection transmitted via the Ixodes tick. Rodents can also serve as reservoirs. Like malaria, there can be human-to-human transmission via contaminated blood transfusions.

Infection pyriform bodies infect erythrocytes. Once inside, they multiply by binary fission, leading to tetrads of merozoties, which escape by lysing the erythrocyte. Eventually, a blood-feeding tick may pick up an infected erythrocyte, enabling transmission to others. Infected cells resemble P. falciparum, but there are no typical malarial pigments or other stages of growth.

Diseases & Clinical Presentation of Babesiosis

Most patients with an intact immune system are asymptomatic or develop mild flu-like symptoms (fevers, mylagias, fatigue). However, patients who are asplenic, immunocompromised or elderly are at increased risk for fatal infection. Symptoms present with an incubation period of 1-6 weeks. Symptoms include general malaise, fever, headache, and other nonspecific symptoms. This presentation may progress to hemolytic anemia with low hematocrit (low RBC count), low hemoglobin, and hemoglobinuria.

Diagnosis of Babesiosis

Babesisis can be diagnosed by PCR detection of babesial 18S rRNA. Giemsa-stained thin blood smears under microscope examination is less reliable than detection via light microscope for malaria. Blood smear may show ring-shaped trophozoites inside red blood cells but can be distinguished from Plasmodium because they do not contain the central brownish deposits (hemozoin) seen with Plasmodium falciparum. Because the trophozoites undergo asynchronous schizogony it can result in the budding of two or four merozoites forming a tetrad of merozoites known as the Maltese cross. The Maltese cross is pathognomonic for Babesia, although it is not always seen on peripheral blood smear.

Treatment & Prevention of Babesiosis

Asytpmtomatic infections are not typically treated unless the Babesia organism persists for >3 months as confirmed by PCR. If Babesia organism is detected in symptomatic patients, treatment is generally done with atovaquone and azithromycin in mild cases. Clinadmycin and quinine are reserved for more severe cases.

Patients living or traveling to endemic areas should take preventive measures including protective clothing with long sleeved and long pants with pants tucked into socks, using tick repellants such as DEET, and examining skin after outside exposure. Removal of ticks within 24 hours of attachment can help prevent infection.
